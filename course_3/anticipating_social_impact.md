> Case Study: LLMs in Kampala
> In a rapidly digitizing Kampala, many universities have embraced LLMs to support student learning. 
> These AI-powered tools help students draft essays, summarize textbooks, translate learning materials, and generate research ideas. 
> For some, particularly those in elite institutions with high-speed internet, LLMs provide an advantage. 
> It allows them to complete assessments efficiently and expand their knowledge beyond traditional textbooks. 
> However, in some less well-funded universities, students may lack access to reliable internet or personal devices, creating a gap in learning opportunities. 
> The increasing reliance on LLMs raises concerns: 
> Does their widespread use increase interdependence and collective knowledge-sharing, or does it deepen inequities between those who can and cannot access them?  

>Educators have also noticed that students who frequently use LLMs tend to lose the ability to think critically, 
> relying on AI-generated responses instead of engaging in deep analysis. 
> Some students even struggle with basic problem-solving, assuming that AI will always provide the correct answers. 
> In an education system where fellowship and community knowledge-sharing are valued, how can LLMs be used in a way that enhances, 
> rather than diminishes, students' intellectual and moral growth? 

> Teachers also worry about the potential loss of human connection in learning. 
> When students rely on AI rather than seeking guidance from their peers or mentors, 
> does this weaken the bonds of relationality and communal learning that have traditionally been central to African education?

Frank Herbert published *Dune* in 1965, anticipating the issue of AI replacing human decision-making more than 60 years ago.
However, this argument assumes that humans ever made decisions in a thoughtful manner.
I would argue that the vast majority of humanity makes decisions based on convenience and herd mentality 
rather than research and critical thinking.

As evidence, I give you the case study above: students *choose* to accept the answers of AI rather than questioning the assumptions and logic behind the answers.
This behavior is identical to the age-old practice of copying tests and paying other students to complete homework.
AI is not creating new behavior, just removing an ethical and/or financial barrier.

I offer social media recommendation engines as a second piece of evidence. 
These deterministic systems influence behavior by equating engagement with positive outcomes,
when, in fact, high levels of engagement often indicate decisions based on herd mentality ("I'll click this because my friend liked it"),
anger, ("That's wrong. I have to do something!"), and
hate ("[That group] is a bunch of [insert derogatory term here]. They're animals! They don't deserve to [fill in the blank].").

Instead of consulting multiple sources to achieve a more nuanced view of a topic, they act on emotion rather than logic.

While it's understandable behavior, the education system has failed humanity for a long time. 
AI is not to blame for inequity in our education system.
